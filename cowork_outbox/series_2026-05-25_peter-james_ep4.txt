ALEX: What the office actually does. The Montgomery County Executive runs an executive branch with about ten thousand merit-system employees. The Executive proposes the annual operating budget — currently around seven billion dollars — and the capital budget. The Executive does not vote on the budget; the County Council does. But the Executive's proposed budget sets the agenda. The Executive signs or vetoes council legislation. The Executive appoints the heads of executive-branch departments — police, fire and rescue, transportation, health and human services, recreation, libraries, environmental protection, permitting services, and so on. The Executive is the public face of the county to the state and to the federal government.

JORDAN: The Executive does not directly run the school system. Montgomery County Public Schools is governed by an elected Board of Education and runs through an appointed superintendent. The Executive funds MCPS through the operating budget — MCPS is the single largest line item, roughly half of total county operating spending — and can push, in budget season, on the level of funding, but cannot direct curriculum, personnel, or facility decisions inside MCPS. That separation is one of the structural features that Peter James's MCPS-restructuring proposal would attempt to change.
