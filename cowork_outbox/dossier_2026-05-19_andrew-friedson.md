## 1. Resolving the person

This is the Andrew M. Friedson who serves on the Montgomery County Council representing District 1 (Bethesda, Chevy Chase, Potomac, North Bethesda) and who launched a campaign for Montgomery County Executive on June 23, 2025 in the Democratic primary for the seat held by Marc Elrich. He is not to be confused with any unrelated person of the same name in the legal, academic, or business literature. [src: Maryland State Archives, https://msa.maryland.gov/msa/mdmanual/36loc/mo/leg/html/msa17973.html ; campaign launch https://marylandmatters.org/2025/06/23/friedson-kicks-off-campaign-for-montgomery-county-executive/]

**Best-estimate birth year:** ~1986. The Maryland State Archives entry shows him earning a B.A. from the University of Maryland in 2008 while serving as student body president in 2007–08, suggesting graduation in his early twenties. He was first elected to the County Council in December 2018, repeatedly described as "the youngest Councilmember in County history," and was elected by his colleagues as the youngest-ever Council President in December 2023. Working back from those markers puts him at roughly age 39–40 as of the June 2026 primary. An exact birth date has **not** been surfaced in this run; flagged in Section 7. [src: https://msa.maryland.gov/msa/mdmanual/36loc/mo/leg/html/msa17973.html ; https://andrewfriedson.com/meet-andrew/]

**Look-back window for Section 3 below:** age 40 − 20 = roughly 20 years, so 2006-present. The biographical record before 2006 (childhood and high school) is intentionally out-of-scope per dossier methodology.

## 3. Deep news history (2006–present window)

Reconnaissance over news, Bethesda Magazine archives, Maryland Matters, Montgomery Perspective, GGWash, WTOP, the Maryland Manual, and campaign sites turned up the following substantive items beyond press-release boilerplate:

- **2007–08, University of Maryland.** Student body president. This is the earliest publicly documented leadership role. No surfaced controversy from this period. [src: https://msa.maryland.gov/msa/mdmanual/36loc/mo/leg/html/msa17973.html]
- **2009–11, Franchot campaign and office.** Managed Comptroller Peter Franchot's campaign organization while still in his early-twenties; bridged into staff work in the comptroller's office. [src: same]
- **2011–16, Maryland 529 / College Savings Plans.** The Maryland 529 college-savings program later experienced highly publicized crises in the early 2020s after parents could not access enrolled funds. Friedson's campaign biography credits him with "a complete restructuring" of the ~$6B program. The 529 board service overlapped with his Comptroller's-office roles; the restructuring credited to him predates the publicly reported 2022–23 calculation-error crisis, which occurred after his board departure. The boundary between his work and the later crisis is worth being careful about; treating him as "responsible for the 2022 crisis" is not supported by the public record. [src: campaign biography; news reporting on 529 crisis post-2022]
- **December 2017.** Announced his candidacy for the District 1 council seat being vacated by Roger Berliner. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/Bethesda-Beat/2017/Andrew-Friedson-Announces-Run-for-District-1-County-Council-Seat/]

## 4. Political track record (voting record by topic)

The Council's legislative database (mococouncil.org / montgomerycountymd.gov press releases) is the authoritative source for individual bill votes. Where this dossier has a verifiable bill number and outcome, it's listed. Where only a press release confirms Friedson's position without naming the roll-call count, that's noted.

**Property tax and county budget**
- Voted **against** property-tax increases proposed by County Executive Marc Elrich in 2020, 2023, 2025, and 2026. Self-positioned as the council's most consistent fiscal-discipline voice. [src: https://andrewfriedson.com/meet-andrew/]
- Authored legislation expanding the county supplement to the Homeowners' Tax Credit (means-tested property-tax credit). Bill number not surfaced in this run; flagged in Section 7. [src: same]
- Voted for the **FY26 Operating Budget** ($7B+, including ~$3.6B for MCPS = 99.8% of the Board of Education's request) **without** a property-tax increase; preliminary agreement was unanimous among 11 councilmembers. [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2025/05/15/council-releases-details-of-fy26-operating-budget/]
- Voted for the **FY25 Operating Budget** ($7.1B; 99.2% of MCPS request) without a tax increase, while preserving reserves above 10.8%. [src: https://www2.montgomerycountymd.gov/mcgportalapps/Press_Detail.aspx?Item_ID=45249]

**Government ethics & transparency**
- **Lead sponsor**, Bill 41-20 (December 2020): mandated disclosure of outside-employment income >$1,000 for non-merit elected and appointed officials; ended unregulated discretionary severance for political appointees; required disclosure of proposed contracts for non-merit positions at appointment. Passed unanimously. [src: https://www2.montgomerycountymd.gov/mcgportalapps/Press_Detail.aspx?Item_ID=28238]
- Led efforts to expand the Office of Inspector General. [src: https://andrewfriedson.com/meet-andrew/]

**Education / MCPS funding**
- Supports robust school funding but has explicitly opposed raising the property tax to do it; feuded publicly with County Executive Elrich over this trade-off. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2024/05/22/elrich-friedson-butt-heads-over-mcps-funding/]
- April 28, 2026: sent letter to MCPS and county police asking them to "develop a strategy" to permanently station police officers in every county high school — sent two days before the April 30 Blake High shooting. The Community Engagement Officer (CEO) program currently in place replaced the older School Resource Officer (SRO) program that had been scrapped after equity concerns. Friedson's letter has revived that debate. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2026/04/30/mcps-police-officers-in-schools/ ; https://wtop.com/montgomery-county/2026/04/theres-a-push-to-put-police-in-every-high-school-permanently-in-montgomery-county/]

**Wootton High School relocation (listener-flagged)**
- October 28, 2025 testimony to MCPS Board of Education: called for **full renovation of the existing Wootton building** rather than relocation; credits Rockville Mayor Monique Ashton and Rockville Councilmember Adam Van Grack with shaping the testimony. Quote: "The idea of relocating Wootton to Crown never even occurred to me until MCPS proposed it… I meant what I said then, and I stand by it now."
- After MCPS Board approved Option H (relocation to new Crown campus in Gaithersburg, effective 2027–2028), Friedson publicly criticized the process for lacking transparency. [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2026/02/10/wootton-high-school-is-becoming-a-major-election-issue/ ; https://bethesdamagazine.com/2026/02/05/wootton-crown-relocation/]
- The county executive does not vote on individual MCPS school-closure decisions — those belong to the Board of Education — but the executive shapes the capital budget that funds school renovation, which is the lever Friedson would have if he wins.

**Housing / land use**
- **Lead sponsor**, ZTA 25-02 ("More Housing N.O.W."): allows duplex/triplex/townhome/apartment housing along county transit corridors (≥3 lanes / >100 ft) and requires 15% workforce-affordable housing in qualifying projects. Passed **8–3** July 22, 2025 after tense Council session. Co-lead: At-Large Councilmember Natali Fani-González. Critics on the Council — most prominently Will Jawando — say it favors developer interests; supporters call it the most consequential pro-housing zoning change in a decade. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2025/07/22/workforce-housing-zoning-passage/]
- **Lead sponsor**, Bill 2-25 (affordable housing; details not fully sourced — flagged in Section 7). Cosponsors include Luedtke, Balcombe, Sayles, and Council President Stewart.
- Sponsored the **FAITH ZTA**, a zoning text amendment making it easier for houses of worship to build affordable housing on their land. [src: https://andrewfriedson.com/meet-andrew/]
- Critic of the county's existing rent-control policy; argues it discourages new construction. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2025/06/06/friedson-rent-control-moco/]

**Climate**
- **Co-sponsor**, Bill 2-26 (Taxation & Fuel-Energy Tax Amendments) with Councilmembers Luedtke and Glass — passed March 18, 2026; expands Green Bank funding for climate-resiliency projects. [src: https://mcgreenbank.org/council-passes-climate-resiliency-legislation-sponsored-by-councilmembers-luedtke-friedson-and-glass/]

**Immigration / sanctuary policy (listener-flagged)**
- **Co-sponsor**, Bill 32-25 — the "Trust Act" (introduced December 2025, passed unanimously). Reaffirms that all residents — regardless of immigration status — can access county services without fear or discrimination. Friedson on the floor: *"We're standing up against the terror that ICE has sown in communities across the country because no family should have to live in fear."*
- **Co-sponsor**, Bill 3-26 — the "County Values Act" (passed unanimously March 24, 2026). Lead sponsor: Kristin Mink. Mandates clear judicial-warrant signage and staff training in county-controlled buildings, and **prohibits immigration-enforcement activities in county-owned parking lots, garages, and vacant lots**. Friedson is one of eight cosponsors. [src: https://www2.montgomerycountymd.gov/mcgportalapps/Press_Detail.aspx?Item_ID=48442&Dept=1]

**Public safety / quality of life**
- Lead co-sponsor (with Luedtke), Bill 13-25 (unlicensed commercial house parties): tightens licensing, raises penalties on for-profit unlicensed residential events. [src: https://www2.montgomerycountymd.gov/mcgportalapps/Press_Detail.aspx?Item_ID=47373&Dept=1]

## 5. Controversies and notable press

- **More Housing N.O.W. backlash.** The 8–3 council vote on July 22, 2025 was preceded by months of community organizing against the bill in single-family neighborhoods. Friedson and Jawando feuded publicly during PHED Committee work sessions. Jawando has accused Friedson of being too close to developer interests. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2025/07/22/workforce-housing-zoning-passage/]
- **Public Election Fund (PEF) opt-out.** Friedson is the only major candidate in the 2026 executive race **not** participating in the county's public financing system; he is using traditional fundraising and reportedly has a commanding cash advantage. Critics say this contradicts his good-government brand (which he himself codified into law via Bill 41-20 in 2020); he argues PEF caps would handicap a competitive countywide campaign. [src: https://mdbaynews.com/2026/01/31/montgomery-county-executive-race-2026-candidates/]
- **Police in schools (April 28, 2026 letter).** Two days before a shooting at Blake High, Friedson publicly asked MCPS and county police to plan permanent officer assignments. Defenders point to the timing as prescient; critics point out the SRO program was scrapped after data showed disproportionate Black/Hispanic student arrests, and a CEO was already on-site at Blake. [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2026/04/30/mcps-police-officers-in-schools/]
- **Endorsements:** IAFF Local 1664 (firefighters — first labor endorsement of the primary, July 2025); Councilmembers Balcombe and Luedtke (April 23, 2026); former candidate David Blair; Greater Greater Washington.

No surfaced ethics complaints, no surfaced FEC complaints, no surfaced lawsuits naming him as a defendant.

## 6. Stance on listener-flagged topics

- **Sanctuary policy on illegal immigration.** Co-sponsored both the Trust Act (Bill 32-25) and the County Values Act (Bill 3-26). He is **not** a candidate who opposes sanctuary-style protections. If the listener wants someone on the ballot who would *roll back* those protections, none of the three leading Democrats (Friedson, Glass, Jawando) fit; all three are co-sponsors of County Values.
- **School funding.** Yes-vote on FY25 (99.2% of MCPS request) and FY26 (99.8%), both without a property-tax hike. Will not raise taxes to fund schools beyond what the existing tax base supports — this is a principle, not a one-time stance.
- **Property tax.** Voted no on every Elrich-proposed property-tax increase (2020, 2023, 2025, 2026). Of the three top Democrats, Friedson is the most consistent "no" on tax hikes.
- **Wootton High School.** On record opposing the Crown relocation; called for full renovation in October 2025 testimony. As county executive, he would not directly reverse the Board of Education's vote, but he would shape the capital budget that funds (or doesn't fund) renovation work at the existing site.
- **Fiscal discipline.** This is his core brand. The legislative record supports it on tax votes. The complication is that "fiscal discipline" *plus* "fully fund schools" *plus* "expand affordable housing" is a hard triangle that the next executive will have to defend in actual budgets — open question whether Friedson can deliver all three when he's the one signing the budget instead of voting on it.
- **MCPS school rating system mechanics.** The county executive does not set the rating system — that's a state/MCPS function. Friedson has not, in the materials surfaced for this run, taken a high-profile public position on the new rating methodology or on the specific Rockville-school scores. Flagged in Section 7.
