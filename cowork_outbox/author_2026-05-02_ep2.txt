JORDAN: Okay. Now the deep dive. School rating system.
ALEX: School rating system. This is going to be longer. The current Maryland school rating system uses a one-to-five-star scale — the press shorthand calls it one-to-four because most schools cluster between one and four, but the formal scale is five stars. It is required by federal law under the Every Student Succeeds Act, which is the 2015 federal law that replaced No Child Left Behind. Every state has to publish school ratings annually. Maryland chose the star system in 2018.
JORDAN: What does each star mean.
ALEX: The star is a composite of about ten indicators, but they collapse into four big buckets. Bucket one is academic achievement — straight standardized test pass rates in English and math. Bucket two is academic progress — how much an individual student's score grew year over year. Bucket three is what the state calls school quality and student success — attendance, school climate surveys, access to advanced coursework. Bucket four, only at the high school level, is graduation rate.
JORDAN: And the problem with that.
ALEX: Two problems the new system is being designed to fix. First, the achievement bucket is weighted around forty-five percent. Schools that serve more low-income students score lower on standardized tests for reasons that have to do with the kids' starting point, not the quality of instruction. So a one-star school is often a high-poverty school, not a bad school. Second, the system gives the same weight to a school regardless of size, so a tiny rural school and a fifteen-hundred-student suburban school get equal treatment, which means small schools' scores swing wildly with one bad year.
JORDAN: How would the new system work differently.
ALEX: This is what the State Board of Education is working through right now. The proposal under consideration has three changes. One — the achievement bucket weight drops from around forty-five percent to around thirty percent, and a new bucket called student readiness goes in at fifteen percent, measuring things like access to advanced coursework, dual-enrollment with community colleges, and certification programs. Two — academic progress, the year-over-year growth measure, gets weighted higher — closer to thirty percent. Three — there is a new accountability indicator for what they are calling opportunity gap, which measures the difference in outcomes between subgroups of students inside the same school. A school that is high-performing for one group and low for another would lose stars.
JORDAN: Will this change the rating of schools in my cluster.
ALEX: Your cluster is School District 005, the Wootton cluster. Under the current system Wootton High is rated four stars. The middle school feeders in your cluster — Cabin John Middle and Robert Frost Middle — are also four stars. The elementary feeders are mixed. Most are three or four stars. The change in weighting would not move Wootton High much because it scores well across categories. Where it would move things is at the elementary level — schools that have wide opportunity gaps would lose a star, and schools that have narrowed those gaps over time would gain one. Specifically, Cold Spring Elementary in your cluster has been improving on academic progress year over year, and would likely gain a star. Stone Mill Elementary has had a wider opportunity gap for the last three years and would likely lose one.
JORDAN: When does this go into effect.
ALEX: The State Board has been collecting public comment through May. Final regulation is expected in July. First ratings under the new system would publish in fall 2027 for the 2026-27 school year. So the very first rating year that uses the new methodology is the year after next, and the first one that would actually inform a school-relocation decision would be the year after that, fall 2028.
JORDAN: So this does not affect the Wootton-Crown decision.
ALEX: It does not. The Wootton relocation to Crown is a 2027-28 building decision based on facility capacity, not on rating. The new rating system would not have helped or hurt that case.
JORDAN: What does this affect for me.
ALEX: Three things. One, when the new ratings come out in 2027, you will see different numbers next to the same schools. Do not be surprised. Two, the new system is going to make it harder for a future school board to argue closing a high-poverty school for low ratings, because the rating will weight achievement less. Three, the County Council and County Executive use ratings to argue for more or less money for specific buildings — a low-rated school becomes a target for capital spending. The new system will redirect some of that capital attention to schools that are showing opportunity gaps, even if their overall rating is high.
