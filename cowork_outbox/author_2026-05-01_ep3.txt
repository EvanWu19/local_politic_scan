JORDAN: Episode 3. What is on the docket.
ALEX: Two big state-level pieces and one Bethesda development item. State first — the Maryland Comptroller's office released a report yesterday on the structural problems facing the state's healthcare workforce. Then we go back to the Supreme Court redistricting ruling we touched in episode one and look at how it specifically lands on Maryland. Then we close with a Pearl Street development in downtown Bethesda that connects directly to the housing fight in your County Executive primary.
JORDAN: Healthcare workforce. What is the headline.
ALEX: Comptroller Brooke Lierman's office published the report on April thirtieth. The headline number — Maryland's healthcare industry employs four hundred twenty-seven thousand people. That is one in twelve working Marylanders. The industry is facing serious worker shortages and an aging workforce, with the report flagging that retirements are outpacing new entrants in nursing, allied health, and direct-care roles.
JORDAN: When you say direct-care, what does that mean.
ALEX: Direct-care workers are the people who provide hands-on personal care — home health aides, certified nursing assistants in nursing homes, residential support staff in disability services. The roles that pay the least and are physically the hardest. Those are the positions where the shortage is sharpest because turnover is highest and entry pay is lowest.
JORDAN: Why is the Comptroller writing about this. That is not a tax thing.
ALEX: The Comptroller in Maryland has a broader portfolio than just collecting taxes. The office issues regular economic analyses, and workforce reports like this one because the state's tax base depends on what industries are growing or contracting. A four hundred twenty-seven thousand-job industry that cannot fill its open positions becomes a fiscal problem for the state — Medicaid spends more on agency staffing, hospitals raise rates, and patient outcomes get worse, which feeds back into more state spending downstream.
JORDAN: How does this hit Montgomery County specifically.
ALEX: Montgomery County is the densest concentration of healthcare employment in the state outside of Baltimore. NIH is here. The county hospital systems — MedStar, Adventist HealthCare, Holy Cross — are major employers. When the state-level workforce shortage shows up here, it shows up as longer wait times for primary care, fewer beds available at peak capacity, and a slower response to mental-health and disability service needs. None of those are abstract. You feel them when you try to schedule.
JORDAN: Does this connect to anyone on my ballot.
ALEX: Indirectly. Wes Moore is the Governor and his office has been pushing workforce-development programs aimed at healthcare. Your federal House district, MD-8, is currently held by Jamie Raskin. Federal healthcare workforce funding flows through agencies in his oversight. None of the County Executive candidates have a healthcare-workforce platform plank as a priority issue, but the next county budget will reflect what the county pays its own social-services staff and how much grant money flows to community-based providers.
JORDAN: So this is one of those slow stories I should know but not vote on.
ALEX: Exactly that.
JORDAN: Voting Rights ruling. We touched it in episode one. What is the deeper story.
ALEX: The U.S. Supreme Court issued a ruling on April twenty-ninth narrowing how states can use race when drawing legislative district maps. The legal mechanic — the Court limited the kinds of evidence courts can consider when assessing whether a district map dilutes minority voting power. In practice, this makes it harder to prove a Voting Rights Act violation in court.
JORDAN: And we said this affects Maryland how.
ALEX: Two ways. First — Maryland's congressional and state legislative maps were last drawn after the 2020 Census. The state's map has been challenged in court before on partisan-gerrymandering grounds, not racial-gerrymandering grounds, but the new ruling changes the legal terrain for the next round. Second — Maryland passed a state-level Voting Rights Act this past General Assembly session specifically to backstop federal protections. The state law is now untested. If a future challenge is brought, the state will be defending it on state-constitutional grounds, which is new territory.
JORDAN: And there is also an immigration angle to the same Court term.
ALEX: There is, but a different case. The Maryland General Assembly passed something this session called the Community Trust Act. The Trust Act would tighten the rules on how Maryland local police can cooperate with federal immigration enforcement — specifically, it would limit the conditions under which police can hold someone solely on an immigration detainer. The bill is on Governor Moore's desk. He has not signed it. Senate Republicans, as reported on April twenty-ninth, have publicly urged him to veto it.
JORDAN: I asked about sanctuary policy on my Wednesday note. This is the state-level version of that question, right.
ALEX: This is the state-level version. And here is where I want to draw a line. There are two different layers of policy that often get lumped together. Layer one is local — what your county police and sheriff do or do not do with federal immigration agents. We covered that in episode one and yesterday's deep dive on Friedson. The Montgomery County Council passed the County Values Act in March, which prohibits ICE enforcement on county-owned property. All three of your County Executive candidates voted yes on that. Layer two is state — what every Maryland city and county police department is allowed or required to do under state law. That is what the Community Trust Act regulates. The Trust Act, if signed, would set a statewide floor — a minimum standard for how local police interact with ICE.
JORDAN: So the Governor's signature is the next thing to watch.
ALEX: That is the next decision point. If Moore signs, the Trust Act becomes state law and applies everywhere in Maryland. If he vetoes, it dies for this session and would have to be re-passed. If he takes no action within the constitutional window, it becomes law without his signature.
JORDAN: When is the deadline.
ALEX: Maryland gives the Governor thirty days from the end of the legislative session to sign, veto, or let lapse. The session ended on April seventh. So the window closes around the first week of May — meaning the next few days. Watch your news feed.
JORDAN: I am going to ask a hard question. If the Trust Act is signed, what changes for me as a Rockville resident.
ALEX: For you specifically — very little operationally. Montgomery County police already operate under one of the more restrictive local frameworks for ICE cooperation in the state. The Trust Act mostly catches up the rest of Maryland to where Montgomery County already is. Where it would matter more is in the rest of the state — places like Frederick County, Carroll County, and parts of the Eastern Shore where the local sheriff has historically had a more cooperative posture with federal immigration enforcement. Statewide uniformity is the policy goal.
JORDAN: That is a different answer than the partisan framing makes it sound.
ALEX: That is one of the consequences of doing the actual reading. The political fight is loud. The actual on-the-ground change for you, in Rockville, is small. The change for somebody in a different Maryland county can be much larger.
JORDAN: Bethesda development. What is happening on Pearl Street.
ALEX: The Montgomery County Planning Board is reviewing a sketch-plan proposal for a three-building, mixed-use development in downtown Bethesda. The site is on Pearl Street. The plan calls for up to five hundred ninety apartments, a public plaza, and a small park. The developer is Promark Partners.
JORDAN: That is in Bethesda. I am in Rockville. Why am I hearing about it.
ALEX: Three reasons. First — this is a direct test of the More Housing N.O.W. zoning bill, the one Andrew Friedson lead-sponsored last year and Will Jawando voted against. The Pearl Street project is exactly the kind of mid-rise, transit-adjacent, mixed-income development the bill was designed to enable. If the Planning Board approves it under the new framework, that is precedent. Other developers will read the signal.
JORDAN: Okay, so this is a real-world test of the housing law.
ALEX: It is. Second — the project includes a workforce-housing component, which is the fifteen-percent affordable requirement that More Housing N.O.W. created. The Planning Board's review will scrutinize whether the affordable share meets the standard. Third — Pearl Street is in the East Bethesda neighborhood, which has been organizing against larger-scale projects. There will be public testimony at the Planning Board hearing, and the testimony will reveal how organized the opposition is. That matters because the same opposition shows up in any future project, anywhere in the county.
JORDAN: When does the Board hear it.
ALEX: The sketch-plan stage is the first review. A specific hearing date had not been set as of the April twenty-ninth reporting in Bethesda Magazine. Sketch plans typically get scheduled within four to eight weeks of submission.
JORDAN: How does this connect to my ballot.
ALEX: It connects directly to County Executive. Friedson's argument is that you cannot solve a housing shortage without legalizing more housing of this exact type. Jawando's argument is that the mid-rise template approved under the new bill favors developers and changes single-family neighborhood character without adequate community input. Glass has been somewhere in between. The Pearl Street decision becomes a real-world demonstration of which framing is winning. If the project goes through cleanly, it strengthens Friedson's case. If it gets bogged down in opposition or denied, it strengthens the case Jawando has been making.
JORDAN: That is useful. So this is one to actually watch.
ALEX: This is one to actually watch. Sketch-plan hearings are public and the testimony is on the public record.
