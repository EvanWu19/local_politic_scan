Three EPA employees who were placed on administrative leave in late April are now alleging that the suspensions were retaliation for raising concerns about how the agency was handling environmental enforcement under the new federal administration. Two of the three are based out of EPA Region 3, which is headquartered in Philadelphia but covers Maryland.
JORDAN: How does this hit Maryland.
ALEX: Region 3 staff handle Chesapeake Bay enforcement. The Chesapeake Bay Program is run out of Annapolis, and its budget is a federal-state cost share. The whistleblowers' complaints, as reported, allege that the agency has been instructed to stop pursuing certain enforcement actions against agricultural runoff sources in Maryland and Pennsylvania. If those allegations are true, the immediate consequence would be that the Bay Program runs slower this year on the agricultural-runoff piece, which Maryland's state agencies would then have to pick up unilaterally.
JORDAN: Which is more cost on the state.
ALEX: Which is more cost on the state, yes. Maryland's Department of the Environment has a budget line for state-level enforcement that is currently around eighteen million a year. If the federal piece is reduced and Maryland wants to maintain the same enforcement level, the state would have to scale that line up. The General Assembly's last budget grew that line by about three percent. Whether to grow it faster will be a fight for the next session, which starts in January 2027.
JORDAN: This is one of those things where the federal cut creates a state cost decision.
ALEX: Exactly that pattern. And it is the same pattern as the food aid story we covered yesterday in episode 4 — federal cuts trickling down to state-level decisions about whether to backfill. Your answer to that question depends on your view of essential versus non-essential spending. Chesapeake Bay enforcement is one of those things that has bipartisan support in Maryland — both the prior Republican governor and the current Democratic governor have grown that line — but the conversation about how big it should be is real.
JORDAN: Which Maryland politicians are on record about the EPA piece.
ALEX: Senator Chris Van Hollen and Senator Angela Alsobrooks both issued statements this week. Van Hollen called for an inspector general investigation. The most concrete proposal in circulation is a term-limit framework: an eighteen-year term for new Justices, with one Justice appointed every two years, replacing the current life-tenure model. The proposal would not affect sitting Justices, only new ones.
JORDAN: This is a thing that has been talked about for years.

The bill needs sixty votes in the Senate to overcome a filibuster, and it does not have them right now. So this is symbolic for the moment. He started Monday. The fundraiser is for ALS Worldwide. The reason this is in a Maryland politics show — Hardesty is doing the ride in part to draw attention to a Maryland-specific bill that died in the last General Assembly session, House Bill 1156, which would have created a state ALS research fund of five million a year for ten years.

ALEX: It died in the House Health and Government Operations Committee. The vote was nine to twelve. Among the no votes were two Montgomery County Delegates whose districts are not yours. Among the yes votes was your delegate Greg Wims. JORDAN: So when there was a vote on a state ALS fund, my district reps were in the yes column.
ALEX: They were. The bill is going to come back next session — Hardesty's ride is part of building public pressure for it. The fiscal note last time was five million a year. The argument from the no side was that ALS research is properly federal, not state. The argument from the yes side was that Maryland's NIH-adjacent research universities, particularly Johns Hopkins, would be the natural home for the funding and would multiply the state dollars with federal grants. This is the kind of small-bore science-funding bill that does not get a lot of press but does represent a category of state spending you might or might not call essential.
JORDAN: This is the part where I have to actually sit with what I said April 28th. I said cut non-essential spending. Is a five-million-a-year ALS fund essential or not.
ALEX: That is a real question and we are not going to answer it for you. The honest framing is — five million a year is roughly seven cents per Maryland resident per year. The argument for is that ALS research is federally underfunded relative to its disease burden, and Maryland is positioned to do this work effectively. The argument against is that the state's job is not to backstop federal science underfunding, that is the federal government's job. Both are honest positions. The only thing I would push you on is that the test for non-essential cannot be is it small — every program is small if you look at it alone. The test has to be is the function appropriately the state's job.

ALEX: It is the frame we are going to keep coming back to. Let us look at the frost story through the same lens.
JORDAN: Frost on Maryland farmers.
ALEX: Late April had two frost nights in a row that came after the budding had already started for stone fruit, certain berries, and early-season vegetables. Maryland Department of Agriculture's preliminary estimate is between eight and twelve million dollars in lost farm income, concentrated on the Eastern Shore and in Frederick and Carroll counties. There are now calls for the state to declare an agricultural disaster, which would unlock state-administered low-interest loans and would qualify Maryland farmers for federal disaster assistance.
JORDAN: Is that essential or not.
ALEX: Same kind of question. The argument for declaring is that Maryland's farm sector is small relative to the state economy — about half a percent of state GDP — but it is concentrated in counties where it is a much larger share of local economic activity. A frost loss at this scale could close some farms permanently. The argument against is that farming is a business and frost is a known business risk. Most other businesses do not get state disaster declarations when their revenue drops.
JORDAN: How does Montgomery County come into this.
ALEX: Montgomery County has the second-largest farm sector in Maryland by acreage, after Frederick. The county's Agricultural Reserve, which is the eighty-thousand-acre protected farming zone in the upper county, was directly affected by the frost. The Council last week — April 30th — passed a resolution requesting the state declare a disaster, with all eleven members in favor. So this was unanimous on your Council. The political question is what happens after the request. The state has to actually declare. Governor Moore has not yet acted.
JORDAN: So Friedson, Glass, Jawando — all three voted yes on the resolution.
ALEX: All three voted yes. Resolutions are not laws, they are formal requests, but they do show the Council's posture. None of them publicly opposed the request. None of them publicly questioned whether agricultural disaster relief is appropriate state spending.
JORDAN: That is unanimous.
ALEX: Unanimous. So if your priority is finding a candidate who is more skeptical of disaster-relief spending, you would not find them on this Council. The Republican candidates for Governor have a range of positions. Dan Cox has historically been skeptical of expanded disaster-relief programs. Chris Bouchat, a state senator from Carroll County which is one of the affected counties, has been actively pushing for the disaster declaration. Ed Hale has not weighed in. So even within the Republican primary there is no clean position.
JORDAN: Good to know it is not a left-right thing.
ALEX: It is more of a rural-versus-urban question than a Democrat-versus-Republican one. Most rural Republican legislators support agricultural disaster relief. Most urban Democratic legislators support it too, in part because losing rural farms means losing rural Democratic votes for the broader farming-protection coalition.
