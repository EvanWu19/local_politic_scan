ALEX: Welcome back. This is Episode 2 of our four-part series on Alec Stone, Democratic candidate for the open District 19 seat in the Maryland House of Delegates. Today's episode is Career.

ALEX: We start at the current job, work backwards. That is the standard order on resumes for a reason — what someone is doing right now tells you the most about who they are right now.

JORDAN: Current job.

ALEX: Alec Stone is currently the Executive Director of two trade associations — concurrently — and a paid advocacy consultant for a third.

JORDAN: Two and a half jobs.

ALEX: Roughly two and a half jobs. The most recent appointment is Executive Director of the National Association of Clinical Nurse Specialists. The acronym is NACNS. He took that role in August of 2025, and he introduced himself to the NACNS membership in a public welcome letter dated September 29, 2025. That letter is on the NACNS website — nacns.org — and it's signed "Alec Stone, MA, MPA, CNE." The clinical nurse specialist profession includes roughly eighty-nine thousand to ninety thousand U.S. nurses, per NACNS's own count. They are advanced-practice nurses who specialize in a clinical area — oncology, mental health, geriatrics, cardiology — and they tend to work at the system level inside hospitals, coordinating care, mentoring other nurses, doing quality-improvement work.

JORDAN: So a real, defined healthcare workforce.

ALEX: A real, defined healthcare workforce. NACNS is the trade association that represents them — running their annual conference, publishing the Clinical Nurse Specialist journal, providing continuing-education credits, running their political-action operation in DC. As Executive Director, Stone is the senior staff person — the person responsible for running the association day-to-day, reporting to a volunteer board.

JORDAN: That's the August 2025 job. There's an earlier one he is still doing.

ALEX: Concurrent. Stone is also the Executive Director of NATSAP — the National Association of Therapeutic Schools and Programs. He took that role in September of 2023. LegiStorm dates it to September 2023; that's the same date used on his current LegiStorm biographical summary. NATSAP represents residential treatment programs, therapeutic boarding schools, wilderness-therapy programs, and outdoor behavioral healthcare programs for adolescents and young adults.

JORDAN: That's a different kind of trade group.

ALEX: It is a different kind of trade group. NACNS is a professional-society — nurses themselves are the members. NATSAP is a member-organization trade association — the schools and programs are the members, not the individual clinicians. NATSAP is the kind of association that does advocacy in state legislatures, defends member programs in front of regulators, sets industry standards, runs an outcomes-research operation, and provides legal and policy support to its members.

JORDAN: Which is where the conflict-of-interest question starts.

ALEX: Which is where the conflict-of-interest question starts, yes. Maryland — like every state — has a regulatory framework for youth residential-treatment facilities. The framework intersects the Department of Health, the Department of Human Services, the Department of Juvenile Services, and the Maryland State Department of Education, depending on what kind of program it is and who's paying. There is genuine state legislative activity in this space across the country. The Trails Carolina case in early 2024 — the wilderness-therapy program in North Carolina where a twelve-year-old client died — accelerated state-level regulatory interest. Utah, Montana, Oregon, Missouri have all passed legislative reforms in this area. Massachusetts has a bill pending. Maryland has not yet had a major fight on this, but if it does in the next four years, Stone — if he wins — is the trade association's executive director sitting in the legislature debating the bill.

JORDAN: That is a recusal problem.

ALEX: That is a recusal problem at minimum. Stone could announce in advance — and any responsible candidate at his point in their career probably would announce in advance — that he will recuse from any legislation directly affecting NATSAP member programs. He has not, to date, said that publicly. We have not seen that statement. If a reporter asks him in a long-form interview before the primary, we'll know more. If he doesn't get asked, the listener should ask at a candidate forum.

JORDAN: And the third job — the consultancy.

ALEX: Stone is the named Advocacy Consultant for the Association of VA Hematology/Oncology — AVAHO. AVAHO is the smaller association of clinicians who treat cancer and blood disorders in the Veterans Health Administration system. The relationship was announced in a February 26, 2024 post on avaho.org titled "Meet Alec Stone, AVAHO's Advocacy Consultant." So he's been the AVAHO consultant since at least February 2024. The piece on the AVAHO site is the most-detailed publicly available biography of him, and it's where we cross-checked his educational credentials in Episode 1.

JORDAN: So three concurrent positions — NACNS, NATSAP, AVAHO. He's running for state delegate on top of those.

ALEX: That is the picture in May 2026. Three concurrent professional engagements. He's running for a state House seat that meets, when in session, ninety days a year from January through April. That schedule is technically compatible with a parallel association-executive career, and it is the schedule most Annapolis delegates run. Many Maryland delegates have a parallel professional life — Charlotte Crutchfield's career was the corporate-counsel and employee-relations track until she joined the legislature. Vaughn Stewart was a public-defender attorney before. Sunil Dasgupta teaches at Universities at Shady Grove and would continue. So a delegate running an association is not unusual.

JORDAN: But the recusal map is the thing.

ALEX: The recusal map is the thing. Open question.

JORDAN: Now go back. The big anchor job — the one that defines the resume.

ALEX: The big anchor job is Oncology Nursing Society. ONS. Stone served as Director of Government Affairs and Advocacy at the Oncology Nursing Society from approximately September 2010 through September 2023 — thirteen years. ONS represents about thirty-five thousand oncology nurses across the U.S. The membership is registered nurses, nurse practitioners, clinical nurse specialists, and others who specialize in cancer care. Stone's role was to run the federal-policy operation — Congressional advocacy, regulatory advocacy at FDA and CMS, the annual Capitol Hill Days member-lobbying event, drafting Congressional testimony, managing the ONS political action committee infrastructure.

JORDAN: What did he actually get done in thirteen years?

ALEX: A few measurable things. One concrete example is the December 2019 ONS Voice piece titled "Two ONS Health Policy Priorities Pass the House Thanks to 2019 Capitol Hill Days." That piece names the two priorities — they were nurse-workforce and palliative-care related bills, the specifics are on the ONS site — and it credits the annual Capitol Hill Days event for moving them through the House.

JORDAN: Capitol Hill Days — explain that.

ALEX: Capitol Hill Days is the annual lobby-day event. The trade association flies several hundred members in from across the country, gives them a one-day policy briefing, splits them into delegations by Congressional district, and sends them to walk the Capitol to meet their members of Congress. Stone ran the ONS version every year for over a decade. There's an ONS Voice piece from 2019 titled "ONS Holds Fourth Annual Capitol Hill Days" — by 2019 it was the fourth annual, so the event under his shepherding launched around 2016. He's a guest on ONS podcast Episode 69, "Nurses Influence Health Policy at ONS's Capitol Hill Days," walking the membership through how the event worked and what it was trying to accomplish.

JORDAN: He also published an academic-journal piece.

ALEX: He published a 2014 piece in Clinical Journal of Oncology Nursing — that's a peer-reviewed nursing journal — titled "Voices of Oncology Nursing Society members matter in advocacy and decisions related to U.S. Health Policy." It's indexed in PubMed. That was his fifth year at ONS and is essentially his thesis statement about why member-driven federal advocacy matters. We didn't try to source the full text, but the title and the PubMed identifier are public.

JORDAN: What about creating Congressional caucuses?

ALEX: The AVAHO biography page credits him with creating two Congressional health caucuses. It doesn't name them. The two most likely candidates — based on the cancer-nursing-advocacy era he was working in — are the Congressional Nursing Caucus, which has a long history, and a more specialized oncology caucus. We're not going to name them definitively without a primary source. It's a useful credential — Congressional caucuses are how members of Congress signal that they care about a particular topic, and they are useful organizing tools for trade-association advocacy. Setting them up is real work.

JORDAN: He also handled Congressional testimony.

ALEX: He drafted Congressional testimony, held legislative briefings, wrote material for the Congressional Record, and set up annual meetings between ONS-member nurses and their members of Congress. That's the bread and butter of an association-government-relations job. The corporate partnerships he developed during the ONS years and earlier — per his AVAHO bio — included GlaxoSmithKline, Procter & Gamble, Colgate-Palmolive, Janssen, Merck, and Pfizer. Those are the big pharma and consumer-products firms whose government-relations operations cooperate with the trade associations.

JORDAN: So ONS 2010 to 2023 is the resume anchor. What's before ONS?

ALEX: Pre-2010 is where the public record gets thinner. His career biographies list — without dating them — work for the American Cancer Society, the American Medical Association, the American Dental Association, the American Library Association, the Deafness Research Foundation, Research!America, and the Federation of American Societies for Experimental Biology, the acronym FASEB. JORDAN: Okay. Let's zoom back to current jobs and walk one specific test. The Trails Carolina case.

ALEX: Yes. In February 2024 — about five months into Stone's NATSAP tenure — a wilderness-therapy program member of NATSAP, Trails Carolina, had a twelve-year-old client die at their North Carolina facility. The case got significant national press. NATSAP responded with a public statement. The statement is on members.natsap.org, in the NATSAP Policy Digest, titled "NATSAP Statement on Trails Carolina."

JORDAN: What did it say?

ALEX: The substance of the statement — and this is paraphrased, not quoted at length — was that NATSAP took the case seriously, that the loss of a child is a tragedy, that NATSAP member programs are committed to safety standards, and that NATSAP-member status carries accountability obligations. The trade association walked carefully — distancing the association from the specific incident without throwing its member program publicly under the bus. That is a pretty standard trade-association posture in a member-organization crisis.

JORDAN: How does Stone come out of that?

ALEX: Honestly — we don't have public-record visibility into his decision-making inside NATSAP. We have the public statement. We don't have the internal email traffic. We don't have on-record interviews with him from that period that go into detail about how the association managed the response. It's another open hour. What we can say is that the response was the typical trade-association response, and the member program was not visibly pulled from NATSAP membership in the months that followed.

JORDAN: That's relevant on substance, but also on character.

ALEX: It's relevant on both. A trade-association executive director who finds out, five months into the job, that a member program has had a child die in their care is being tested. The test is whether the executive director prioritizes the member's interest or the public-safety interest, and where they draw the line. Stone's approach — based on the published statement — was the standard one. Whether the listener wants more than the standard one out of a candidate for the legislature is a substantive question.

JORDAN: Okay. Let's flip to the resume positives. What does this career give him for the legislature?

ALEX: Several things, actually. The first is straightforward — he has spent twenty-plus years learning how a legislative chamber actually works. Reading bills line by line, drafting amendments, talking to committee staff, watching markups, building coalitions across members from different parties and different geographies. That set of skills doesn't transfer one-to-one from Capitol Hill to Annapolis — the floor procedure is different, the committee structure is different, the calendar is different — but the underlying competence is real and unusual for a first-time candidate.

ALEX: That is the implicit "ready on day one" argument the campaign is making. Most first-term delegates spend their first session learning the basics — how does a bill move through the Maryland General Assembly, who are the gatekeepers, what does the rules committee actually do. Stone is claiming he doesn't need that year. Whether that translates into faster legislative output is an empirical question the listener will only be able to test if he wins.

JORDAN: The second positive.

ALEX: The second positive is policy substance, specifically healthcare. Stone has spent two-plus decades working on cancer-care reimbursement, nurse-workforce policy, drug pricing, FDA regulatory practice, CMS coverage decisions. The Maryland General Assembly has substantive healthcare jurisdiction — the Prescription Drug Affordability Board, the Health Services Cost Review Commission rate-setting framework, Medicaid eligibility decisions, the Maryland Health Insurance Exchange, the Maryland Health Care Commission. On those topics specifically, Stone is the most-credentialed candidate in the open-seat field. Not the most-credentialed in the District 19 race overall — Senator Kramer has multiple legislative sessions of healthcare-committee work — but among the new candidates competing for the third seat, Stone has the deepest substantive healthcare reservoir.

JORDAN: Third positive?

ALEX: Third positive is coalition-building experience. Running annual Capitol Hill Days for thirteen years means he has personally trained roughly several thousand non-professional advocates how to walk into a Congressional office and make a case. He has worked across the aisle on health policy — cancer-care advocacy was always a bipartisan posture. He has done coalition work with corporate partners and with patient-advocacy partners simultaneously. That is, again, an unusual reservoir of practical experience for someone running for the legislature for the first time.

JORDAN: Okay. Let's name the resume problem clearly before we close.

ALEX: The resume problem is the conflict-of-interest map. Stone is a sitting executive director of two trade associations and a paid consultant for a third. NATSAP's industry — youth residential treatment — is regulated in part by state legislatures. NACNS's industry — advanced-practice nursing — is regulated in part by state-level scope-of-practice law. Maryland is an active legislative state on both of those topics, periodically. If he wins, he is going to face votes that affect the bottom line of organizations he simultaneously runs as their senior staff person. The question is whether he announces a recusal protocol in advance, and which kinds of legislation it covers. The listener has every right to ask that question at a candidate forum.

JORDAN: Anything from a financial-disclosure standpoint?

ALEX: Maryland's personal financial disclosure for legislators is more thorough than many states' but is filed annually only by sitting legislators. As a non-incumbent candidate, Stone has not been required to file one. If he wins, his first personal financial disclosure as a sitting delegate will be filed within thirty days of swearing-in next January and will include his trade-association compensation, board memberships, and any direct financial interests. The state campaign-finance reports through the Maryland State Board of Elections — those are public. The 2026 pre-primary report is due May 26, 2026. That report will show his fundraising base — how much, from whom, in what amounts. We're recording this two weeks before that report is due. We'll know more then.

ALEX: Career as one paragraph. Alec Stone has spent more than twenty years working in DC federal-policy advocacy for healthcare and biomedical-research organizations. He spent his core decade-plus running federal advocacy at the Oncology Nursing Society. His career gives him an unusual amount of practical legislative experience for a first-time candidate, real substantive depth on federal healthcare policy, and a serious conflict-of-interest map he has not yet publicly addressed.
