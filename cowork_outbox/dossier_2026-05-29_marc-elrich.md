**Office sought (2026):** Montgomery County Council, At-Large (4 seats open, 17 Democratic filers) [src: https://www.thebanner.com/politics-power/local-government/montgomery-county-final-candidates-2026-V2WGULT3HRFTTBGNLHKUVLS6CI/]
**Party:** Democratic
**District:** Countywide (MoCo)
**Primary:** June 23, 2026
**Filed:** Campaign committee established Sept 10, 2025; public launch rally Dec 4, 2025, McGinty's Public House, Silver Spring [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2025/09/10/elrich-establishes-campaign-committee-for-council-at-large/] [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2025/11/18/elrich-campaign-county-council-at-large/]

## Step 1 — Identity resolution

The Marc Elrich on the 2026 At-Large ballot is the **same** Marc Elrich currently serving his second term as Montgomery County Executive (sworn in Dec 2018; re-elected by a 32-vote margin in 2022 after a recount) [src: https://marylandmatters.org/2022/08/24/elrich-wins-montgomery-county-executive-primary-race-by-32-vote-margin/]. He is term-limited from a third Executive term because Montgomery County voters passed Question B in November 2024, reducing the Executive's term limit from three terms to two [src: https://mocoshow.com/2025/11/19/marc-elrich-to-launch-campaign-for-at-large-montgomery-county-council-seat/]. He is running for the At-Large Council seat he previously held (2006–2018) — this is a return to a body he served on for three terms before becoming Executive.

There is no ambiguity in the candidate filing: the registered campaign committee, the kickoff venue (Silver Spring), and the policy platform (sanctuary, rent stabilization, MCPS funding) all align with the incumbent Executive.

## Step 2 — Biographical foundation

### A. Demographics
- **Born:** November 2, 1949, in Washington, D.C. [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Elrich]
- **Age on primary day (June 23, 2026):** 76
- **Current residence:** Takoma Park, Montgomery County, MD (long-time resident; lived in the city through his entire 19-year run on the Takoma Park City Council)
- **Family:** Publicly disclosed family information is sparse. He has not made his marital status or children a campaign theme. The county Executive bio page and Wikipedia entry both omit spouse/children details, which is itself notable for an elected official in his 70s.
- **Religion / ethnicity:** Jewish background (commonly noted in profiles; e.g., FAIR.org's 1988 profile on Takoma Park's "socialist suburb" describes him in this context) [src: https://fair.org/home/a-socialist-in-wapos-suburbs/]
- **Languages:** English; no public claim of additional languages.

### B. Socioeconomic
- **Compensation:** As County Executive, statutory salary in the ~$240K range for FY2026 (county salary schedule; specific figure on the OHR salary publication). Prior 12 years on County Council carried a councilmember salary roughly in the $130–145K range over his tenure.
- **Net worth & holdings:** Not publicly itemized beyond the Maryland State Ethics Commission annual financial disclosures, which Executive Elrich files each April. No major business ownership or active stock-trading record has surfaced in press coverage; he is widely characterized as one of the less personally wealthy figures in MoCo elected politics — a contrast he himself has drawn against opponents (notably David Blair in 2018 and 2022, who self-funded millions).
- **Home ownership:** Long-time Takoma Park homeowner (referenced in local coverage of his housing-policy work).
- **Conflicts of interest:** No public ethics findings against him as Executive. Has been openly antagonistic to developer interests and the local FOP — those tensions are political, not financial.

### C. Education
- **K–12 (all Montgomery County Public Schools):** McKenney Hills Elementary → Montgomery Hills Junior High → Albert Einstein High School (Kensington, MD) [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/exec/bio/marc_elrich.html]
- **Undergraduate:** University of Maryland, College Park — Bachelor's degree [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/exec/bio/marc_elrich.html]
- **Graduate:** Johns Hopkins University — Master of Arts in Teaching [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/exec/bio/marc_elrich.html]
- No honorary degrees publicly listed; serves on the Board of Advisors at the Universities at Shady Grove [src: https://shadygrove.usmd.edu/about-usg/leadership-governance/board-of-advisors/marc-elrich]

### D. Professional history (reverse chronological)
- **Dec 3, 2018 – present:** Montgomery County Executive (1st term Dec 2018–Dec 2022; 2nd term Dec 2022–Dec 2026). Won 6-way 2018 Democratic primary by 77 votes over David Blair; won 2018 general with 64% [src: https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/md-politics/montgomery-county-executive-election-marc-elrich-nancy-floreen-and-robin-ficker/2018/11/06/725772c0-d86d-11e8-aeb7-ddcad4a0a54e_story.html]. Won 2022 Democratic primary in a Blair rematch by 32 votes (55,504 to 55,472) after a manual recount of ~140,000 ballots [src: https://marylandmatters.org/2022/08/24/elrich-wins-montgomery-county-executive-primary-race-by-32-vote-margin/].
- **Dec 2006 – Dec 2018:** Montgomery County Council, At-Large (three consecutive 4-year terms). Lead sponsor of the 2013 county minimum-wage hike to $11.50 and of two later bills to raise it to $15 (the January 2017 version was vetoed by then-Executive Ike Leggett; a follow-on bill passed and was signed) [src: https://www.marcelrich.org/factcheck].
- **1989 – 2006:** Elementary-school teacher, Rolling Terrace Elementary, Takoma Park (Montgomery County Public Schools). Rolling Terrace was at the time one of the county's lowest-income schools — Elrich has repeatedly cited this 17-year stretch as the experience that shaped his views on housing instability and child poverty [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/exec/bio/marc_elrich.html].
- **1987 – 2006:** Takoma Park City Council (19 years; ten 2-year terms by Takoma Park's biennial cycle). Authored the city's sanctuary ordinance limiting cooperation with federal immigration enforcement; strengthened the city's rent-stabilization law; pushed Takoma Park's "nuclear-free zone" declaration; sponsored domestic-partnership recognition; protected day laborers [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Elrich] [src: https://fair.org/home/a-socialist-in-wapos-suburbs/]
- **Early 1980s:** Tenant and neighborhood organizer in Takoma Park, focused on housing and equitable development — his on-ramp into electoral politics [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Elrich].

There is no five-year unaccounted-for stretch. The continuous teacher-plus-councilmember overlap from 1989 to 2006 is unusually long and is worth flagging on-air as the formative period.

## Step 3 — Deep news history (lookback window: age 76 → 56 years, used full ~45-year electoral lookback to 1980)

### Early-1980s organizing → 1987 first elected
Takoma Park in the early 1980s was a magnet for left-progressive activists and the city went through a wave of tenant-rights and nuclear-free-zone organizing. Elrich was one of the figures who came out of that scene. FAIR's 1988 critique "A Socialist in WaPo's Suburbs" treats him as a representative figure of the Takoma Park progressive bloc that the Washington Post was, in FAIR's view, dismissing [src: https://fair.org/home/a-socialist-in-wapos-suburbs/]. He won his first Takoma Park council seat in 1987.

### Nineteen years on the Takoma Park City Council (1987–2006)
Across ten consecutive 2-year terms he:
- Authored Takoma Park's sanctuary ordinance limiting local cooperation with federal immigration enforcement (Takoma Park was one of the earliest U.S. municipalities to formalize this) [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Elrich].
- Strengthened the city's rent-stabilization law — Takoma Park was a rent-control outlier in Montgomery County and Elrich is associated with successive tightenings [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Elrich].
- Backed Takoma Park's nuclear-free-zone declaration, an emblematic 1980s peace-movement ordinance [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Elrich].
- Sponsored domestic-partnership recognition (pre-Goodridge, pre-Obergefell).
- Backed protections for day laborers and immigrants.
Critics writing retrospectively (e.g., CleanSlateMoCo's "Seeds of Stagnation & Decline" essay) argue these years also entrenched a slow-growth politics that has since shaped the entire county [src: https://www.cleanslatemoco.com/seeds-of-stagnation-decline-part-2-marc-elrichs-early-years-in-takoma-park-city-government/938]. Supporters cite the same period as foundational for sanctuary, rent control, and tenant protections.

### Council At-Large, 2006–2018
- 2013: Lead sponsor of bill raising the county minimum wage to $11.50/hr [src: https://www.marcelrich.org/factcheck].
- 2016–17: Twice lead sponsor of legislation to raise the minimum wage to $15. A January 2017 bill cleared the Council and was vetoed by Executive Ike Leggett; a revised follow-on bill passed [src: https://www.marcelrich.org/factcheck].
- Rent control: Did not introduce a rent-control bill while on the Council "because he did not have the votes" — Montgomery Perspective's reporting confirms he played a supporting rather than sponsoring role on this issue during the Council years [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2023/07/20/winners-and-losers-of-the-rent-control-battle/].
- Generally aligned with the progressive bloc on land-use, transit, and labor issues; often the lone "no" on development approvals he viewed as too dense for surrounding infrastructure.

### 2018 Democratic primary for County Executive
A six-candidate race (Elrich; David Blair; Roger Berliner; George Leventhal; Rose Krasnow; Bill Frick). Elrich edged Blair by 77 votes county-wide; a recount was triggered, and after the recount Elrich was certified as the Democratic nominee [src: https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/md-politics/elrich-edges-out-blair-in-democratic-primary-for-montgomery-county-executive/2018/07/09/1ecd14c8-7ef1-11e8-b0ef-fffcabeff946_story.html] [src: https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/md-politics/after-recount-marc-elrich-remains-democratic-executive-nominee/2018/07/23/0873689c-8ec5-11e8-b769-e3fff17f0689_story.html].

### 2018 general election
After Elrich won the primary, Council member Nancy Floreen switched her registration from Democrat to unaffiliated and mounted an independent challenge backed by parts of the business community [src: https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/md-politics/as-elrich-captures-democratic-nomination-business-leaders-look-to-floreen/2018/07/09/19fde078-8390-11e8-8f6c-46cb43e3f306_story.html]. Republican Robin Ficker also ran. Final tally: Elrich 64%, Floreen 19%, Ficker 16% [src: https://wtop.com/montgomery-county/2018/11/2018-election-montgomery-county-executive-marc-elrich-nancy-floreen-robin-ficker/].

### 2022 Democratic primary rematch with David Blair
A near-tie. With all ballots counted, Elrich led by 42 votes (39.20% to 39.17%) [src: https://marylandmatters.org/2022/08/07/blair-trailing-by-42-votes-will-request-recount-in-montgomery-county-executive-race/]. Blair requested a manual recount of all ~140,000 ballots. The recount netted Blair +10 and Elrich +7. Final certified margin: 32 votes (Elrich 55,504 — Blair 55,472) [src: https://marylandmatters.org/2022/08/24/elrich-wins-montgomery-county-executive-primary-race-by-32-vote-margin/]. Blair conceded.

### November 2024: voters tighten Executive term limits
Question B passed, reducing the County Executive term limit from three terms to two. This is the proximate reason Elrich is not running for a third Executive term and is instead returning to the At-Large Council ballot [src: https://mocoshow.com/2025/11/19/marc-elrich-to-launch-campaign-for-at-large-montgomery-county-council-seat/].

### 2025–2026: launch of the At-Large campaign
- Sept 10, 2025: Established the campaign committee for Council At-Large [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2025/09/10/elrich-establishes-campaign-committee-for-council-at-large/].
- Nov 18, 2025: Email announcement to supporters [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2025/11/18/elrich-campaign-county-council-at-large/].
- Dec 4, 2025: Kickoff rally at McGinty's Public House, Silver Spring [src: https://mocoshow.com/2025/11/19/marc-elrich-to-launch-campaign-for-at-large-montgomery-county-council-seat/].
- Did not respond to Greater Greater Washington's 2026 endorsement questionnaire; GGW declined to back him, writing that "after nearly 40 years in Montgomery County politics, they hope voters will give him a well-deserved retirement from public service" [src: https://ggwash.org/view/102768/morrison-ashman-stewart-fani-gonzalez-luedtke-goldberg-silvestre-sayles-mcnulty-for-montgomery-county-council].
- Gov. Wes Moore's May 2026 Montgomery County endorsement slate notably did not list Elrich; Moore endorsed a different set of Council and General Assembly candidates [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2026/05/11/moore-endorses-moco-council-general-assembly-candidates/].

## Step 4 — Political track record as County Executive

### Recurring fights with the County Council
Even after a clean executive-branch sweep in 2018 and 2022, Elrich has been repeatedly characterized in MoCo coverage as a chief executive who *runs against* his own council. Montgomery Perspective's "This is Why Other Politicians Dislike Marc Elrich" (Sept 2024) catalogues his pattern of publicly criticizing Council majorities on budget and zoning fights [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2024/09/30/this-is-why-other-politicians-dislike-marc-elrich/].

A representative example: in May 2024 Elrich publicly criticized the Council — particularly Andrew Friedson — for "underfunding schools," even though Elrich's own recommended FY25 operating budget included less MCPS spending than the Council's spending plan [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2024/05/22/elrich-friedson-butt-heads-over-mcps-funding/].

### MCPS funding (FY24 → FY27)
- FY25 (2024 budget cycle): Elrich's recommended budget for MCPS was below the Council's eventual appropriation; Elrich nonetheless framed the Council as cutting schools [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2024/05/22/elrich-friedson-butt-heads-over-mcps-funding/].
- FY27 (current cycle, FY27 begins July 2026): Elrich's recommended budget *fully* funded the MCPS request. The Council in its straw vote allocated approximately 80% of Elrich's recommendation (after initially allocating 60%), leaving the Board of Education ~$35M short of its request [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/news/montgomery-update-county-council-approves-fiscal-year-27-straw-vote-honoring-mcpd-during-police-week-noyes-library-renovations-begin] [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2026/05/12/the-budget-just-got-harder/].

For a Rockville Wootton-area parent following the closure/relocation question (listener focus item), the relevant inference is that Elrich is now positioned as the *more* school-funding candidate relative to the Council majority — a flip from the 2024 framing.

### School Resource Officers (SROs)
In his FY22 budget, Elrich moved to eliminate the SRO program in MCPS high schools, replacing in-school officers with a different model. FOP Lodge 35 publicly opposed the move; the union framed it as "DEFUNDING the police has started in MoCo" [src: https://wjla.com/news/local/montgomery-county-fop-upset-with-elrich-plan-to-eliminate-school-resource-officer-program] [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/bethesda-beat/schools/elrich-says-he-will-remove-police-officers-from-schools/]. SROs were ultimately removed and replaced with Community Engagement Officers stationed outside schools.

### Rent stabilization
After never being able to introduce a rent-control bill during his 12 years on Council, Elrich publicly pressed for one as Executive. His credible veto threat is generally credited with blocking a weaker compromise bill in 2023 and pushing the Council toward a stronger version of the eventual rent-stabilization law [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2023/07/20/winners-and-losers-of-the-rent-control-battle/]. In July 2025 he publicly called for tightening that law further [src: https://montgomeryperspective.com/2025/07/14/elrich-wants-to-tighten-rent-control/]. Whether the Banner's 2025 reporting shows the law is "working" is contested [src: https://www.thebanner.com/economy/growth-development/rent-stabilization-rent-control-development-UDE54MRA7JDGZKI3QM6PAKN4TA/].

### Immigration / sanctuary framework
Elrich's sanctuary politics predate the county role: he authored Takoma Park's sanctuary ordinance in the 1980s–90s. As Executive he has defended MoCo's existing limits on cooperation with ICE. He has not publicly broken with the framework on the Council level — though several At-Large opponents this cycle have called for tightening *or* loosening it (see listener-focus answers below for the specific contrast among the five+ At-Large Democrats).

### Endorsements
- Greater Greater Washington (2026 Council slate): declined to endorse [src: https://ggwash.org/view/102768/morrison-ashman-stewart-fani-gonzalez-luedtke-goldberg-silvestre-sayles-mcnulty-for-montgomery-county-council].
- Gov. Wes Moore (May 2026 MoCo slate): not endorsed [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2026/05/11/moore-endorses-moco-council-general-assembly-candidates/].
- Labor / SEIU / teachers' union endorsements for the 2026 cycle: pending as of the May 28 snapshot; he has historically had strong MCEA (Montgomery County Education Association) ties given the teacher background, and labor allies generally on minimum wage.

## Step 5 — Controversies and notable press

**No criminal record, no ethics finding.** What there is, instead, is a long record of *political* friction:

1. **The "anti-business" framing.** Since 2018 the Greater Washington business press, the County Chamber, and several development-industry voices have called him obstructionist on growth. He rejects the framing; supporters counter that his slow-growth instincts are policy, not animus. This is the persistent through-line.

2. **MCPS funding "lighter than the Council, louder than the Council" (FY25).** The May 2024 Friedson clash is the cleanest documented instance of Elrich criticizing the Council for cutting schools while his own recommended budget allocated less to MCPS than the Council eventually did [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2024/05/22/elrich-friedson-butt-heads-over-mcps-funding/]. This is the kind of contradiction a careful listener should ask him about directly.

3. **FOP Lodge 35 SRO fight (FY22).** Public union-vs-Executive break over removing police from schools [src: https://wjla.com/news/local/montgomery-county-fop-upset-with-elrich-plan-to-eliminate-school-resource-officer-program]. Has shaped police-union opposition to him in every cycle since.

4. **Question B (Nov 2024) — a structural rebuke.** Voters tightened the term limit specifically while a sitting Executive (Elrich) was finishing his second term. The referendum's framing as a check on entrenched executives is itself a piece of negative context [src: https://mocoshow.com/2025/11/19/marc-elrich-to-launch-campaign-for-at-large-montgomery-county-council-seat/].

5. **GGW's unusually pointed non-endorsement.** Greater Greater Washington explicitly called for his retirement in the 2026 slate. That language is unusual in MoCo endorsement copy [src: https://ggwash.org/view/102768/morrison-ashman-stewart-fani-gonzalez-luedtke-goldberg-silvestre-sayles-mcnulty-for-montgomery-county-council].

6. **The "lone wolf" pattern.** Maryland Matters' Dec 2018 "Does Marc Elrich Have an Inner Circle?" profile remains a touchstone — the piece argued his governing style runs more solo than other recent Executives [src: https://marylandmatters.org/2018/12/17/does-marc-elrich-have-an-inner-circle/]. Allies say independence; critics say isolation.

## Step 6 — Stance on listener-flagged topics

**Pre-political biography of incumbents (Friedson, Glass, Jawando, and by extension Elrich himself).** Elrich's pre-political biography is the *most* publicly documented of the five+ At-Large Democrats running — 17 years teaching in Takoma Park, 19 years on the Takoma Park Council before his County Council career even started. The listener can fact-check him on biography in a way that is harder with the newer candidates.

**Judicial branch races / Register of Wills / Clerk of Circuit Court / BoE-beyond-at-large / Republican alternatives.** Not directly within Elrich's portfolio as a Council candidate; he has no public position on the judicial races. Worth noting on-air only as: "Elrich is not on any of those ballots — the listener has separate decisions there."

**Ben Kramer's full historical voting record (2007 → present) on consumer protection, judiciary, immigration, education funding.** Kramer is a state senator, not a county figure — outside Elrich's record. But Elrich's *Council* votes (2006–2018) overlap Kramer's *Delegate-then-Senate* tenure; on minimum wage (state preemption fights), sanctuary, and tenant protections the two have generally been aligned.

**Is Friedson actually the fiscal-restraint candidate measured by line-item votes?** Elrich and Friedson have publicly clashed on the MCPS-funding line specifically; on tax-rate votes Friedson has consistently been the most fiscally cautious of the Council Democrats, while Elrich (as Executive) has proposed budgets that test the charter spending limit. The 2024 dispute is the cleanest data point for the listener: Elrich's *recommended* budget for MCPS was *below* what the Council majority eventually appropriated [src: https://bethesdamagazine.com/2024/05/22/elrich-friedson-butt-heads-over-mcps-funding/]. So on schools-as-a-line-item, the framing flips.

**Wootton High School closure/relocation and timeline.** Not within an Executive's direct authority — that is an MCPS Board decision, with the Executive's lever being the overall MCPS appropriation. Elrich's FY27 budget fully funded MCPS' request [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/news/montgomery-update-fy27-recommended-operating-budget-highlighted-effort-fully-fund-mcps-request-local-security-concerns-wake-attacks-michigan-virginia-gov-moores-efforts-help-purple-line-corridor-businesses]; the Council straw-voted to ~80% of that [src: https://www.montgomerycountymd.gov/news/montgomery-update-county-council-approves-fiscal-year-27-straw-vote-honoring-mcpd-during-police-week-noyes-library-renovations-begin]. So a Wootton-area parent who wants schools funded at the requested level finds Elrich aligned with that ask in this cycle.

**Among the five+ At-Large Democrats, which one (if any) actually opposes the existing sanctuary framework?** Elrich is the author of the framework's earliest county-area version (Takoma Park, 1990s). He is the *least* likely of the field to oppose it. We don't have a sourced public statement from another At-Large candidate breaking with sanctuary as of this dossier date — that gap should be filled before Episode 4.

**New MCPS rating methodology and per-school grades for Rockville schools.** The methodology and Rockville-specific school grades are an MCPS Board / MSDE matter; Elrich has not weighed in publicly. Bottom line: unclear / no record yet.
