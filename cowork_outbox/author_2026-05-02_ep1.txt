JORDAN: What does it actually do.
ALEX: Three substantive pieces. One — it creates a state-level cause of action. Meaning if a Maryland jurisdiction draws a district map that dilutes a minority community's voting power, you do not have to sue under the federal Voting Rights Act anymore. You can sue under the Maryland one in a Maryland court. That matters because the federal courts, after the Louisiana ruling, just got narrower about the kinds of claims they will hear.
JORDAN: So Maryland built itself a parallel courthouse.
ALEX: A parallel cause of action, yes. Two — it locks in language-assistance requirements. If a precinct has a meaningful concentration of voters who speak a particular language at home, the local board has to provide ballot materials in that language. The federal law has been doing some of this already, but the state version covers smaller jurisdictions that the federal trigger misses. For Montgomery County specifically, the population thresholds in the state act will pull in additional Korean and Amharic ballot coverage in some Wheaton and Silver Spring precincts.
JORDAN: That is tangible.
ALEX: Three — it requires preclearance for changes to election procedures in any jurisdiction with a documented history of voter suppression. That includes a few rural Eastern Shore counties and some Baltimore-region jurisdictions. They cannot, for example, close polling places or move them long distances without state Attorney General sign-off. None of those preclearance jurisdictions are in Montgomery County, so that piece does not directly touch your June primary.
