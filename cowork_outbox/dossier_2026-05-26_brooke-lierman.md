# Brooke E. Lierman — Candidate Dossier

**Office sought:** Maryland Comptroller (statewide constitutional office)
**Party:** Democratic
**Status on 2026 ballot:** Incumbent, seeking second four-year term
**Primary status:** UNCONTESTED in the June 23, 2026 Democratic primary (no other Democrat filed). General-election opponent: Sonya Dunn (R).
**District relevance to listener:** Statewide office — appears on every Maryland Democratic primary ballot, including the listener's Rockville/Montgomery County ballot.

## 1. Identity resolution

There is exactly one publicly-known Brooke Lierman in Maryland politics: **Brooke Elizabeth Lierman**, the sitting 34th Comptroller of Maryland (sworn in January 16, 2023), former state delegate for House District 46 (Baltimore City, 2015–2023), and a civil-rights attorney at the Baltimore firm Brown, Goldstein & Levy. There is no name confusion to resolve. [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooke_Lierman] [src: https://msa.maryland.gov/msa/mdmanual/08conoff/comptroller/html/msa17084.html]

### C. Professional history (reverse chronological)

- **2023 – present:** **Comptroller of Maryland.** First woman independently elected to a Maryland constitutional office. Sworn in January 16, 2023, after defeating Bowie Mayor Tim Adams in the 2022 Democratic primary and Republican Barry Glassman in the general election.
- **2015 – 2023:** **Maryland House of Delegates, District 46** (Baltimore City). Served on the Appropriations Committee 2015–2019; Vice Chair, Transportation & Environment Subcommittee of Appropriations; Vice Chair, Oversight Committee on Pensions; member, Capital Budget Subcommittee (2019).
- **c. 2010 – 2022:** **Counsel, Brown, Goldstein & Levy LLP** (Baltimore civil-rights firm). Practice covered workers'-rights litigation, disability-rights cases, Section 1983 civil-rights litigation, commercial contract disputes, and Randolph-Sheppard Act cases representing blind vendors. Part of the team representing blind students seeking accessible technology at colleges and universities. Recognized on Maryland Super Lawyers Rising Stars list every year since 2013.
- **2008 – c. 2010:** Federal judicial clerkship (post-law-school).
- **Pre-law (2001 – 2005):** Worked in Washington for several years before law school — staff for Sen. Mark Pryor (D-AR) and at the Brookings Institution have been reported in her own campaign biography. [src: https://brookelierman.com/meet-brooke/]
- **Pro bono throughout legal career:** Homeless Persons Representation Project (expungements); representation of community associations before Baltimore's Board of Liquor License Commissioners.

Lierman has not been publicly reported as personally wealthy. ## 3. Career-long voting and policy record (House of Delegates 2015–2023)

The listener's standing focus categories — sanctuary policy / immigration, fiscal restraint, MCPS / Wootton, sanctuary on the consumer-protection / judiciary axis — do not all map cleanly onto a Baltimore City delegate's portfolio. But Lierman built a clear, narrow, **transportation-and-environment** legislative identity in the House, plus a secondary identity on **transit equity and disability access** drawn from her law practice. Specific legislation that became law or passed the House under her sponsorship:

- **2019 — Polystyrene ban (HB 109 / "Foam Ban").** Lierman was the lead House sponsor of the bill banning expanded-polystyrene food-service products statewide. The bill passed both chambers and became law on May 28, 2019, without Gov. Larry Hogan's signature. Maryland was the first U.S. state to enact a statewide foam ban. [src: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brooke_Lierman]

- **2020 — Plastics and Packaging Reduction Act.** Lierman introduced this bill to ban single-use plastic bags statewide and require a 10-cent paper-bag fee. The bill passed the House 95–37 in the 2020 session but did not clear the Senate before COVID-19 cut the session short.

- **2020 — Maryland Metro/Transit Funding Act.** A Lierman-introduced bill to increase dedicated funding for Maryland Transit Administration bus and rail maintenance passed the House 95–36; it was a companion to her broader push for a permanent MTA capital-funding stream.

- **2019 — County Veto on Toll Roads.** Lierman introduced legislation giving counties authority to block toll-road, highway, or bridge construction without local consent — aimed in part at the Hogan administration's proposed Beltway/I-270 expansion. The bill passed but Hogan vetoed it. The veto override fight became a notable 2020 session story.

- **2019 — Statewide bicycle infrastructure funding.** Lierman bill passed the House but was vetoed by Hogan.

- **Throughout 2015–2022:** Lierman was a consistent vote with the House Democratic majority on the Kirwan / Blueprint for Maryland's Future education-funding package, the 2021 climate-solutions law, expansion of voting access, and the cannabis-legalization referendum.

## 4. Comptroller record (Jan 2023 – present)

This is the most recent and most directly relevant material for a primary-eligible incumbent.

### Major initiatives delivered or in progress

- **Maryland Tax Connect Portal — launched Feb 1, 2024.** A complete replacement of the comptroller's business-tax filing system, the first major rebuild of that infrastructure in roughly 30 years. Phase 1 (business taxes) is live; the individual-tax phase was promised "this term" of office. [src: https://www.marylandcomptroller.gov/about/brooke-lierman/state-of-the-agency/modernize-technology.html]

- **IRS Direct File enrollment for Maryland — announced Sept 4, 2024; live for 2025 filing season.** Maryland joined the federal IRS Direct File program, allowing roughly 700,000 Marylanders with simpler returns to file federal taxes for free directly with the IRS. This is the kind of consumer-financial-access work that aligns with Lierman's pre-political civil-rights practice. [src: https://www.marylandcomptroller.gov/content/dam/mdcomp/md/media/2024/09-04-24-Comptroller-Federal-State-Partners-Announce-MD-Joining-IRS-Direct-File-2025.pdf]

- **Board of Public Works Spending & Contracts Dashboard — launched Oct 2025.** Implements 2024 legislation (HB 877) requiring the Comptroller's Office to build a public dashboard of all Board of Public Works contract approvals. The dashboard exposes the ~$30.7 billion in state spending approved since 2023. The Comptroller sits on the three-member Board of Public Works alongside the Governor and State Treasurer, so this dashboard is in part transparency on her own votes. [src: https://bizmonthly.com/news/government/2025/10/maryland-board-of-public-works-launches-new-spending-and-contract-dashboard/] [src: https://nottinghammd.com/2025/10/23/new-dashboard-gives-marylanders-direct-access-to-30-7-billion-in-state-spending/]

- **Website overhaul — March 2025.** A full rebuild of marylandcomptroller.gov for accessibility (Section 508 compliance), mobile responsiveness, and search. This continues a pattern of Lierman prioritizing the customer-service / accessibility surface of the agency.

- **Revenue forecasting & fiscal warnings.** Lierman has signed the quarterly Board of Revenue Estimates forecasts that have framed Maryland's roughly $3 billion 2024–2025 structural budget gap. Her public posture has been to call repeatedly for both spending discipline AND new revenue (a "shared-sacrifice" framing), as opposed to Gov. Wes Moore's more cuts-first early posture. The two are publicly aligned but the tonal difference has been noted in Annapolis press coverage.

### Posture on Board of Public Works

The three-seat BPW — Governor Moore (D), Treasurer Dereck Davis (chosen by the General Assembly), and Comptroller Lierman (D, elected) — approves all state contracts above statutory thresholds. Lierman has used the seat to push for more contract-disclosure detail, to question contracts on minority-business-enterprise compliance, and to slow several procurement items pending more documentation. None of her BPW dissents have so far been on items that map directly to the listener's MCPS / Wootton / sanctuary priorities.

### What she has NOT done as Comptroller (relevant to listener priorities)

- She has not used her office to weigh in on Montgomery County Public Schools facility decisions (including Wootton). The Comptroller does not have direct authority over MCPS, but the office can — and prior comptrollers have — issued statements on local school-finance matters; Lierman has stayed in her lane.
- She has not made immigration enforcement or sanctuary policy a focus. The Comptroller has no enforcement role here, so this is expected.
- She has not done a major independent performance audit of MoCo county government. (The Office of Legislative Audits inside the Department of Legislative Services, not the Comptroller, runs the regular state audits.)
