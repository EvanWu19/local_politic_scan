ALEX: Voting record, by topic. Six topics. Property tax, schools, housing, climate, immigration, and public safety.

ALEX: Property tax first because it is the topic you have flagged most. Friedson has voted against every county property-tax-rate increase that Marc Elrich has proposed. Four of them. 2020, 2023, 2025, and 2026. The mechanics — the County Council has to approve the property-tax rate as part of the annual operating budget. Elrich, as County Executive, proposes a budget that often includes a higher rate. The Council can approve or reject. Friedson has been a no on every one. He has also been the loudest no, in the sense that he is the councilmember who shows up at the work session with the spreadsheet.
JORDAN: He votes no, and he makes the case publicly.
ALEX: Both. He has not just been a yes-or-no vote, he has been the person asking why a particular department needs another six percent. He authored the expansion of the county's supplement to the state Homeowners' Tax Credit, which is a means-tested property-tax credit. That helps lower-income homeowners specifically. The bill number for the supplement expansion is one I have been trying to surface and I have not yet — it appears in his campaign material as a Friedson-led effort but I have not pulled the council legislative-database link. We will get it for the next pass.
JORDAN: That is honest. What about the budget when he is on the yes side.
ALEX: The most recent example is FY26. The Council passed a seven-billion-dollar operating budget that included three point six billion for MCPS, which was 99.8 percent of what the Board of Education had asked for. There was no property-tax increase in it. Friedson voted yes on the budget. Every other councilmember also voted yes — that one was unanimous on the preliminary agreement. The previous year, FY25, was the same dynamic — 99.2 percent of the MCPS request, no tax increase, Friedson yes.
JORDAN: So when he can fund schools without raising the rate, he votes yes.
ALEX: When he can fund schools without raising the rate, he votes yes. The trade-off is reserves and capital. The way you fund schools at almost-100-percent-of-request without a rate hike is by drawing down on reserves and slowing capital projects. He has been comfortable doing that. The county's reserves are above their 10.8-percent floor, which is the policy minimum, but they are not as high as some councilmembers want. That is a real second-order argument inside the Council.
JORDAN: Schools.
ALEX: Schools, second topic. Friedson supports MCPS funding generally. He has voted yes on every operating budget since he joined the Council. Where he has differed publicly with Elrich is the question of whether to raise the property tax to add to MCPS funding. Elrich has wanted to. Friedson has not. The two of them have feuded on this in committee work sessions and in interviews going back to 2023.
JORDAN: Specific votes.
ALEX: April 28th, 2026, this past Monday — Friedson sent a letter to MCPS Superintendent Thomas Taylor and to the County Police Chief, asking them to develop a strategy to permanently station police officers in every county high school. The letter was sent two days before the firearm incident at Blake High School. The position itself was on his record before the incident. We have noted that twice in earlier episodes; we are not going to re-litigate the timing. The substance is — he wants permanent uniformed officers in high schools. The current program is the Community Engagement Officer model, which assigns one officer to a cluster but not full-time on-site. The previous program, the School Resource Officer or SRO program, put officers full-time in schools but was scrapped in 2021 over data showing disproportionate Black and Hispanic student arrests. Friedson's position would effectively bring back something closer to SROs.
JORDAN: That is a real change.
ALEX: That is a real change. Glass and Jawando have both criticized it. Glass has not committed to a specific alternative. Jawando has explicitly opposed bringing back permanent in-school officers, citing the disparate-impact data. So this is one of the cleanest splits among the three Democrats running.
JORDAN: Wootton.
ALEX: Wootton is the listener-flagged story that you have asked about three times. October 28th, 2025 — Friedson testified before the MCPS Board of Education and explicitly called for full renovation of the existing Wootton building rather than the relocation to Crown High School in Gaithersburg. He credited Rockville Mayor Monique Ashton and Rockville Councilmember Adam Van Grack with shaping that testimony. His direct quote — the idea of relocating Wootton to Crown never even occurred to me until MCPS proposed it; I meant what I said then, and I stand by it now. After the Board approved the relocation, he criticized the process — specifically a lack of transparency in how the Board got to the decision.
JORDAN: As County Executive, would he reverse it.
ALEX: He cannot directly reverse a Board of Education vote. The County Executive does not have authority over school facility decisions; the elected MCPS Board does. What the County Executive does control is the capital budget — the dollars that fund renovation or new construction of MCPS buildings. So if Friedson wins, and he wanted to reopen the Wootton question, his lever would be funding the renovation of the existing building in the next capital budget. Whether the Board would accept that and reverse its own vote is a separate question. Realistically, the political cost to MCPS of reversing a relocation decision after the parents who supported it have planned around it is high. So this is closer to a long-shot than a likely outcome.
JORDAN: Honest answer.
ALEX: Honest answer. Housing, third topic.
JORDAN: This is where he is most controversial.
ALEX: This is where he is most controversial. Lead sponsor of the More Housing N.O.W. zoning text amendment, ZTA 25-02. The bill allows duplex, triplex, townhome, and apartment housing along county transit corridors — defined as streets that are at least three lanes or more than a hundred feet wide — and requires fifteen percent workforce-affordable units in qualifying projects. Passed the Council 8 to 3 on July 22nd, 2025. Co-lead sponsor was At-Large Councilmember Natali Fani-González.
JORDAN: Three no votes.
ALEX: Three no votes. Will Jawando was one of them. He has accused Friedson publicly of being too close to developer interests. The other two no votes were Kristin Mink and Marilyn Balcombe — and Balcombe, interestingly, has now endorsed Friedson for County Executive despite voting no on his housing bill. That is the kind of working relationship that survives a single bill disagreement.
JORDAN: The argument against the housing bill.
ALEX: The argument against from the no side is that the bill effectively upzones single-family neighborhoods adjacent to transit corridors and was insufficiently negotiated with the affected communities. The argument from the yes side is that Montgomery County's housing affordability problem requires increasing supply, that workforce-affordable mandates inside the bill protect against pure market-rate development, and that the bill is the most consequential pro-housing zoning change in the county in a decade. Both sides are honestly held.
JORDAN: He sponsored other housing bills too.
ALEX: He has been lead or co-lead on Bill 2-25, which is an affordable housing bill whose specific scope I have not been able to confirm — that is on my open-questions list. He has also been publicly critical of the county's existing rent control policy, arguing it discourages new construction. That is a position-not-a-vote — there has not been a recent council vote on rent control to record him on.
JORDAN: Climate.
ALEX: Co-sponsor of Bill 2-26, the Taxation and Fuel-Energy Tax Amendments. Co-sponsors were Dawn Luedtke and Evan Glass. Passed March 18th, 2026. Expands funding for the Montgomery County Green Bank, which finances climate-resiliency projects. The Green Bank is a quasi-public entity — county-chartered, but operates with a separate board and does its own deal-making. The bill increased the funding stream that flows from a portion of the energy tax into the Green Bank. So Friedson, Luedtke, and Glass are all on record as supportive of expanded climate finance through that vehicle.

Bill 3-26, the County Values Act, passed unanimously March 24th, 2026. Lead sponsor was Kristin Mink. Mandates clear judicial-warrant signage and staff training in county buildings, and prohibits immigration-enforcement activities in county-owned parking lots, garages, and vacant lots. Friedson is one of eight co-sponsors. So on sanctuary policy, he is a yes vote on the strongest pieces the county has passed.
