The change being discussed is opening primaries to unaffiliated voters — letting people who have not joined either party participate in either party's primary.
JORDAN: Why would Maryland do that.
ALEX: Two arguments. The pro side, which Moore is now leaning toward, is that Maryland has roughly nine hundred thousand registered unaffiliated voters who currently cannot vote in primaries. In a state where the Democratic primary effectively decides most general elections, that is a lot of people locked out of the consequential vote. The con side is that party primaries are supposed to be the party's choice of nominee, and people who have not joined the party should not get to influence that choice.
JORDAN: I just joined the party so I could vote in the primary. If primaries become open, did I waste that step.
ALEX: You did not waste it. Even under the most aggressive open-primary proposal under discussion, Democrats would still vote in the Democratic primary; the change would let unaffiliated voters also opt in. So your registration still gives you the same right to vote that you had before. What it would do is dilute the relative weight of registered Democrats in the outcome — meaning candidates would have to appeal to a broader audience.
JORDAN: Does that change which candidates win.
ALEX: It tends to favor candidates who can pitch across the unaffiliated middle. In Montgomery County's County Executive race, the conventional take is that Friedson would benefit most from open primaries because he polls best among independent-leaning voters. Glass and Jawando have a tighter Democratic base. So if open primaries became law before the next election, the dynamic would shift somewhat toward Friedson. But we are talking about a change Moore is signaling for a future session, not for June.
JORDAN: When earliest could it apply.
ALEX: Earliest realistic implementation would be the 2028 cycle, given the legislative timeline. Maryland's legislature meets ninety days a year; an open-primary bill would have to pass the 2027 session, then the State Board of Elections would need at least a year to implement it, then it would apply to 2028 primaries. So this does not affect Andrew Friedson's June 23rd race. It might affect his next race if he wins this one.

JORDAN: Okay. Microgrid.
ALEX: Montgomery County Department of General Services announced a microgrid is coming online at the Derwood transit operations facility. A microgrid is a small-scale local power system — solar, battery storage, and the ability to disconnect from the regional grid during outages. The Derwood facility is where the county's bus fleet, including the new electric buses, charges overnight. The microgrid means those buses keep charging even if the wider grid goes down.
JORDAN: This sounds like good infrastructure but I do not see the politics.
ALEX: The politics is in how it is paid for. The Derwood microgrid is funded with a combination of federal Inflation Reduction Act tax credits, county capital budget, and a long-term power-purchase agreement with the developer. The county did not have to take on bond debt for it because of how the financing was structured. The reason this is interesting — there has been a running argument on the Council about whether the county is overusing bond debt for capital projects. The Derwood project is being held up by Friedson and others as a model of how to do capital without bonding.
JORDAN: So this is a Friedson talking-point case.
ALEX: It is a Friedson talking-point case. He has cited the Derwood project specifically in two recent campaign appearances as an example of his fiscal approach — get federal money where possible, structure private partnerships where possible, only bond when you have to. Will Jawando has not directly criticized the Derwood project but has pushed back generally on long-term power-purchase agreements as a category, arguing they create off-balance-sheet liabilities. Evan Glass has supported the project without making it a campaign theme.
JORDAN: That is the kind of substantive difference I want.
ALEX: Then you should know that the Derwood model — federal tax credits plus private developer plus county capital — is going to be repeated for several more facilities. The question of whether Montgomery County does six more Derwood-style microgrids in the next four years is one Friedson is essentially saying yes to, Jawando is qualifying, and Glass is saying yes to without strong opinion.
