ALEX: Maryland Matters reported on April twenty-eighth — work has officially begun on a new state school rating system that will replace the current one-to-four-star system. The current system rates each school in the state on a one-to-five-star scale. The change has been driven by an argument that has been building for years — that the star system unfairly penalizes schools that serve high concentrations of low-income students.
JORDAN: How does it penalize them.
ALEX: The star system weighs standardized test scores heavily. Schools serving more low-income kids tend to score lower on standardized tests, partly because those tests measure things like vocabulary exposure and at-home academic support that track with household income. When test scores get a heavy weight in the rating, those schools end up with one or two stars even when teachers and principals are doing strong work on growth and engagement. The criticism is that the rating becomes a label that follows the building around, makes it harder to recruit teachers, and reinforces the segregation it should be exposing.
JORDAN: So the new system is going to weight things differently.
ALEX: That is the design question Maryland is now opening up. The state's Accountability and Implementation Board, working with the Department of Education, is going to develop new criteria. The principle behind the new system, as quoted in the reporting, is that schools should be judged more on growth and improvement and less on raw test-score levels. Whether that holds up when the actual rubric is published is the open question.
JORDAN: When does the new system go live.
ALEX: The reporting did not give a specific launch date, only that work to develop it has begun. Realistically, a project of this scale takes at least a school year to design and roll out. You should expect the current star system to still be in effect when ratings are next published, with the new system likely landing in the 2026 to 2027 cycle at the earliest.
JORDAN: Why does this matter for the County Executive race.
ALEX: Two ways. First, county leaders use school ratings to argue for more or less funding for specific buildings — a one-star rating becomes a budget hammer for facility renovation, a five-star rating becomes a justification to leave well enough alone. If the criteria change, the politics of which schools get capital priority changes too. Second, school ratings are tied up in the same Wootton conversation we walked through in episode one. The local County Council and County Executive react to what gets rated low or high. JORDAN: What does the Sheriff in Montgomery County actually do day-to-day.
ALEX: This is one of those positions that confuses people because Maryland sheriffs do not have the same job description across counties. In Montgomery County, the Sheriff's Office is responsible for serving court papers — civil summonses, protective orders, eviction notices — and providing courthouse security. They do not patrol the streets the way the Montgomery County Police Department does. The MCPD handles patrol, traffic, and most criminal investigations. The Sheriff's role is narrower.

Second, courthouse security is the Sheriff's responsibility, which means county court access depends on whether the office is functioning. A no-confidence vote from the union of the people who actually staff that office is a flag that something inside it is not working.
